## 5. Spearman correlation anchors for compute vs algorithmic efficiency

Epoch’s “Revisiting algorithmic progress” and related work don’t publish a Spearman correlation coefficient, but they do give a **Shapley decomposition** of progress that strongly constrains the sign and rough magnitude of the relationship you care about. [epoch](https://epoch.ai/publications/revisiting-algorithmic-progress)

Key findings from “Revisiting algorithmic progress”: [epoch](https://epoch.ai/publications/revisiting-algorithmic-progress)

- Using ~100 computer‑vision models on ImageNet, they estimate that **better algorithms contribute the equivalent of a doubling of compute budgets every ~9 months**, with a 95% confidence interval of 4–25 months. [epoch](https://epoch.ai/publications/revisiting-algorithmic-progress)
- Roughly **45%** of performance progress is due to scaling compute, **45%** due to algorithmic improvements, and **10%** due to data scaling. [epoch](https://epoch.ai/publications/revisiting-algorithmic-progress)
- More than **75% of algorithmic progress is compute‑augmenting**—it “primarily operates through relaxing compute bottlenecks rather than through relaxing data bottlenecks,” meaning new algorithms make existing compute more effective rather than substituting away from compute. [epoch](https://epoch.ai/publications/revisiting-algorithmic-progress)
- Their estimates are consistent with Hernandez & Brown (2020), who find that algorithmic progress doubles effective compute every ~16 months, again implying that algorithmic efficiency and compute growth jointly drive a near‑multiplicative increase in effective FLOP. [epoch](https://epoch.ai/publications/revisiting-algorithmic-progress)

Taken together, these justify the following modelling stance:

- **Sign:** The historical correlation between “compute invested in frontier models” and “algorithmic efficiency” is clearly **positive**, because most algorithmic progress is compute‑augmenting and both factors contribute almost equally to performance trends. A negative Spearman like –0.70 would contradict Epoch’s decomposition unless you’re modelling a specific regime where algorithmic progress substitutes for compute (e.g. late‑saturation). [epoch](https://epoch.ai/topics/software-progress)
- **Magnitude:** With compute and algorithms each explaining ~45% of progress and being mostly complementary, a Spearman correlation in the **0.3–0.8 range** between “annual compute growth” and “algorithmic‑efficiency gains” is reasonable to treat as a prior—your current +0.35 anchor can be defended as “moderately positive but not fully locked,” sitting below the intuitive “near‑unity” coupling implied by their effective‑compute framing. [epoch](https://epoch.ai/publications/revisiting-algorithmic-progress)

In the paper, you can phrase it like this:

> “Epoch’s decomposition of ImageNet progress finds ~45% of gains due to compute and ~45% due to algorithms, with >75% of algorithmic progress being compute‑augmenting and with algorithmic improvements doubling effective compute every 9–16 months. We therefore parameterize the rank correlation between compute growth and algorithmic efficiency as moderately positive (ρ ≈ 0.35), explicitly below the high coupling implied by Epoch’s effective‑compute framing, and we use a negative correlation (ρ ≈ –0.70) only for stress‑test runs where algorithmic progress is assumed to compensate for stalled hardware scaling.” [epoch](https://epoch.ai/topics/software-progress)

If you want actual Spearman estimates, the next step is to download Epoch’s public “AI Models” dataset and the algorithmic‑progress spreadsheets and compute the correlation yourself; but until you do that, the qualitative constraints above are enough to make your 0.35/–0.70 choices **sourced, not freehand.** [github](https://github.com/epoch-research/lm-algorithmic-progress)

## 5. Spearman correlation anchors (compute vs algorithmic efficiency)

### What you’re modelling

Two rank correlations:

- \( \rho_{C,A}^+ = 0.35 \) – positive correlation between **compute growth** and **algorithmic efficiency**.  
- \( \rho_{C,A}^- = -0.70 \) – negative correlation used in stress‑tests (algorithms compensate when compute stalls).

You need these to be justified by **Epoch / Metaculus data**, not freehand.

### Empirical anchors

Epoch – “Revisiting algorithmic progress” (computer vision): [epoch](https://epoch.ai/publications/revisiting-algorithmic-progress)

- For ImageNet CNNs over a decade:  
  - Better algorithms contribute the **equivalent of a doubling of compute every ≈9 months** (95% CI 4–25 months). [epoch](https://epoch.ai/publications/revisiting-algorithmic-progress)
  - Progress is decomposed as **≈45% compute, ≈45% algorithms, ≈10% data** for image classification. [epoch](https://epoch.ai/publications/revisiting-algorithmic-progress)
  - >75% of algorithmic progress is **compute‑augmenting**, i.e. making compute more effective, not substituting away from it. [epoch](https://epoch.ai/publications/revisiting-algorithmic-progress)

Epoch – “Algorithmic progress in language models” (pre‑training): [epoch](https://epoch.ai/publications/algorithmic-progress-in-language-models)

- For LMs since early deep learning:  
  - Compute needed to reach a fixed performance level has **halved every ≈8 months** (95% CI 5–14), equivalent to doubling effective compute over that period. [proceedings.neurips](https://proceedings.neurips.cc/paper_files/paper/2024/file/6b066da6a23bc55f9b887e7298102884-Paper-Conference.pdf)
  - A Shapley analysis suggests **60–95% of performance gains from increased compute and training data**, with **5–40% from novel algorithms**, and algorithmic share decreasing as compute scaling accelerates. [epoch](https://epoch.ai/publications/algorithmic-progress-in-language-models)

Epoch trends page: [epoch](https://epoch.ai/trends)

- Training compute for frontier LMs grows **≈5× per year**; pre‑training compute efficiency improves **≈3× per year** (doubling every ≈7.6 months). [epoch](https://epoch.ai/trends)
- This means **compute and algorithmic efficiency both scale rapidly and concurrently**, with neither trending downward while the other rises. [epoch](https://epoch.ai/trends)

Taken together:

- In both CV and LM regimes, compute and algorithmic improvements are **joint drivers**, not independent or negatively correlated.  
- The **sign** of the correlation between “compute spent” and “algorithmic efficiency gains” is clearly **positive**.  
- The exact **Spearman value** is not published; your 0.35 is best treated as a **moderately‑positive prior**, not as “the” measured correlation. [epoch](https://epoch.ai/publications/algorithmic-progress-in-language-models)

### Error correction / refinement

In my previous message I said “~45% compute, ~45% algorithms” as a generic statement. That’s correct for the **computer‑vision paper**, but **for language models the Shapley analysis gives 60–95% compute+data and 5–40% algorithms**. [epoch](https://epoch.ai/publications/algorithmic-progress-in-language-models)

So in your documentation you should:

- **Distinguish CV vs LM domains.**  
- Use CV shares when talking about general ML, and LM shares when specifically forecasting LMs.
