ASI Arrival Forecast: Comprehensive Evidence Audit and Recalibration Report
The current architecture of the public ASI Arrival Forecast repository exhibits severe methodological vulnerabilities that mathematically distort the predicted arrival distributions of Artificial General Intelligence (AGI) and Artificial Superintelligence (ASI). Foremost among these failures are the naive additive combinations of highly correlated parameters, the reliance on critically contaminated benchmarks (such as SWE-bench Verified), and a systemic under-appreciation of physical infrastructure frictions compared to algorithmic capabilities.
This document serves as an exhaustive, mathematically rigorous audit of the 14 fundamental variables governing the stage-gated Monte Carlo model. Utilizing empirical data extracted from 2025–2026 intelligence reports, econometric models, and frontier laboratory evaluations, every input has been systematically recalibrated. Where evidence is robust, precision is defined; where the evidentiary basis is weak, uncertainty is explicitly quantified rather than artificially suppressed.
Q1: METR Task-Horizon Decomposition and the Double-Count Problem
The repository currently utilizes a task-horizon dampening exponent of 0.5. This parameterization is mathematically indefensible and introduces severe collinearity into the Monte Carlo sampler. METR’s Time Horizon 1.1 (TH1.1) framework, updated in early 2026, indicates that the 50% task horizons of frontier models have been doubling every 131 days (approximately 4.3 months) since 2023. However, this metric is overwhelmingly a downstream consequence of effective compute growth, rather than an independent driver of capability.   
If effective compute grows at roughly 10× per year—combining physical scale and algorithmic efficiency—and the capability threshold required to double a task horizon scales predictably, treating task horizon as an independent additive variable guarantees double-counting. Furthermore, METR evaluations utilizing highly customized agent scaffolding (such as Claude Code and Codex) do not demonstrate statistically significant improvements over base models paired with standard ReAct or Triframe scaffolds. For instance, in bootstrap samples, specialized scaffolds only beat general scaffolds marginally, indicating that the task-horizon growth is dominated by the base model's raw capabilities. The underlying trend of 131-day doubling times maps directly to the exponential growth in parameter count and training FLOPs.   
The analysis strongly recommends adopting Formulation A (treating task horizon purely as a downstream signal rather than an independent additive variable). If the repository architecture rigidly forces the use of a dampening exponent, it must be set to 1.0 (complete dampening/exclusion) to prevent inflating the predicted pace of progress through double-counting.

C. Monthly Probability Modelling Plan
To generate rigorous monthly probabilities for the final outputs (T∈{AGI,internal ASI,public ASI}), the methodology must aggressively eliminate artificial smoothing.
Sampling Generation: The model must run a minimum of N=100,000 Monte Carlo simulations to ensure absolute stability in the deep right tail. Use Latin Hypercube Sampling (LHS) structured via a Gaussian copula to correctly distribute the +0.35 correlation between compute and algorithmic efficiency without violating marginal bounds.
Binning Mechanism: Do not utilize Kernel Density Estimation (KDE). KDE inherently forces mass backward over the time axis ("back-bleeding"), which produces mathematically incoherent probabilities (e.g., assigning a 0.5% chance that AGI arrived in 2024). Extract exact discrete counts per calendar month.
Outputs Required:
P(T=YYYY−MM): Calculated strictly as the count of simulation samples falling in that specific discrete month divided by N. Adjacent months may possess identical probabilities.
P(T≤YYYY−MM): The cumulative sum of the monthly discrete probabilities up to the evaluated month.
P(T>year): Calculated as 1−P(T≤December of target year).
Interval Generation: Derive the 50% interval (25th to 75th percentiles) and 90% interval (5th to 95th percentiles) directly from the empirical Cumulative Distribution Function (CDF).
Precision Restraints: Probabilities must be rounded to the nearest 0.1%. Week-level or day-level precision is scientifically indefensible given the inherent volatility of both algorithmic breakthroughs and physical data center deployments.
