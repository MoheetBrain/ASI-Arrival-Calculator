ASI Arrival Forecast: Comprehensive Architecture, Modeling Strategy, and Repository Implementation
Executive Summary
Forecasting the arrival of artificial superintelligence (ASI) requires moving beyond theoretical speculation and anchoring predictions in empirical data, structural modeling, and rigorous uncertainty quantification. The objective of this report is to architect a transparent, mathematically sound, and publicly auditable repository for the ASI Arrival Forecast project. The target variable is the first arrival date of capability-and-agency ASI, defined as an artificial intelligence or agentic system capable of outperforming top human teams across all critical cognitive workflows—including AI research, software engineering, scientific discovery, and long-horizon autonomous tasks—without the requirement of subjective consciousness.
Recent empirical developments in 2025 and 2026 have fundamentally altered the forecasting landscape. The contamination of SWE-bench Verified, the acceleration and subsequent stabilization of autonomous task horizons, the evidence of diminishing returns in early AI R&D proxy tasks like the NanoGPT speedruns, and the implementation of United States export controls via the AI Diffusion Rule all inject complex, non-linear dynamics into ASI timelines. Furthermore, leading structural models, such as the AI Futures model and its derivatives, have recently shifted their median arrival dates toward the early 2030s by explicitly modeling the friction of recursive self-improvement and data bottlenecks.   
To capture these dynamics accurately, this report proposes a Hybrid Structural Monte Carlo architecture. By combining explicitly specified causal stages with parameters fitted from historical time-series data, the model avoids the false precision of pure machine learning approaches while mitigating the subjective biases of manual expert estimation. The following sections provide a complete blueprint for the repository, encompassing data ingestion schemas, stage-gated mathematical architectures, statistical validation protocols, and a concrete development roadmap. The resulting framework will yield a highly resilient, dynamically updating forecast capable of delivering month-level probabilistic precision regarding the arrival of ASI.
Recommended Model Architecture
The optimal forecasting pipeline must sequence the development of ASI through consecutive, dependent stage-gates. Progress in AI capabilities is not a monolithic leap; rather, it is a sequential unlocking of bottlenecks where the output of one capability serves as the input to accelerate the next. The recommended architecture evaluates seven distinct stages. For each stage, the model relies on specific input variables, defined probability distributions, threshold criteria, and mathematical equations.

Repo File-Format Schemas
To prevent structural decay and the "garbage-in, garbage-out" phenomenon, the repository relies on rigidly defined, plain-text schema files. By using YAML for assumptions and CSV for structured data, non-experts can audit the inputs natively within the GitHub interface. Furthermore, Pydantic models in the Python pipeline will enforce type-checking and bounds-checking upon every pull request.
1. forecast_inputs/base_forecast_inputs.yaml
This file defines the probability distributions passed into the Monte Carlo sampler. Every parameter requires explicit bounds, a distribution type, and citations tracing back to the evidence notes.

Statistical Method Recommendation
Choosing the correct statistical architecture is the most critical decision in the repository. Evaluating an unprecedented, singular future event (the arrival of ASI) precludes methodologies reliant purely on historical training data. A comparison of the requested options is detailed below.
Manual Expert-Assumption Monte Carlo: Rapid and highly auditable. However, it risks encoding the author's subjective priors ("I think it will take 3 years") without dynamically responding to new benchmark data.
Bayesian Model: Excellent for updating prior beliefs with new data, but generally lacks the capacity to model multi-stage causal dependencies without complex structural networks.
Survival / Hazard Model: Used in biostatistics for "time-to-event" forecasting. While mathematically elegant, it assumes underlying continuous risk functions that fail to capture the step-function nature of AI algorithmic breakthroughs.
Ensemble Forecast Model: Aggregates various separate models (e.g., averaging Metaculus with Epoch trends). Good for reducing variance but lacks internal causal logic, making it a black box.
Time-Series Extrapolation: Fits exponential or logistic curves to benchmark scores. Extremely vulnerable to paradigm shifts (e.g., test-time compute scaling altering previous scaling laws) and benchmark saturation.
Causal Graph / Structural Model: The most intellectually rigorous, defining exact relationships (Compute → R&D → ASI). However, running deterministic structural models generates false precision without uncertainty distributions.
ML Model Trained on Historical Benchmarks: Trains an LSTM or Transformer to predict future scores. Verdict: Overkill and fundamentally flawed. Deep learning cannot predict phase transitions (like the onset of recursive self-improvement) because there is no data of ASI in the training set (N=0).
Fully Trained Model Predicting ASI Date: Mathematically invalid for a singular, unobserved future event.
Hybrid Model (Manual Assumptions + Statistical Fitting): Recommended. This approach defines the causal chain manually (as a structural causal graph), allowing experts to encode logical stage-gates (e.g., you cannot have RSI before you have Automated Coders). However, the parameters governing the transition between these nodes (e.g., the exact task-horizon doubling time) are statistically fitted from the trend_inputs.csv using Bayesian inference.
Conclusion for Repository Implementation:
Use the Hybrid Model. In v0.1, implement a basic Structural Monte Carlo. By v0.3, introduce Bayesian updating using PyMC or SciPy curve-fitting to dynamically adjust the means (μ) and variances (σ) of the YAML parameters based on the time-series CSV data. This provides supreme auditability while remaining highly responsive to empirical shocks.
ML vs Manual Modelling Verdict
Question: Should I train a model for this task, or should I use a manually specified probabilistic model?
Answer: You must strictly avoid training a machine learning model to predict the ASI date. The application of neural networks or supervised machine learning to forecast a singular, unprecedented historical singularity is an epistemological category error. There is no historical training data for "Arrival of ASI." An ML model trained on historical AI benchmarks will merely learn to extrapolate past benchmark curves, entirely failing to comprehend the systemic phase transitions introduced by recursive self-improvement or physical energy grid constraints.
Instead, you must use a manually specified probabilistic model (Structural Monte Carlo). If statistical fitting is required, limit it to estimating specific parameters (e.g., fitting a logistic regression to METR time horizons to find the doubling rate). The overarching architecture of how those parameters interact to produce ASI must be manually encoded as mathematical equations (as outlined in the Model Architecture section).
Monte Carlo Simulation Guidance
Proper execution of the Monte Carlo engine is essential to prevent false precision and ensure reproducibility across disparate computational environments.
Simulation Volume and Convergence
10,000 simulations: Sufficient for local debugging and "fast" runs to ensure the causal logic holds.
100,000 simulations: The ideal target. This volume stabilizes the median and accurately populates the 5th and 95th percentiles (the 90% confidence interval) without succumbing to noise in the tails.
1 million to 2 million simulations: Unnecessary. At this scale, the computational overhead increases linearly, but the forecast accuracy does not improve. The remaining uncertainty in predicting ASI is dominated by structural epistemic uncertainty (the model equations being imperfect), not aleatoric sampling error.
Convergence Checks: Implement Batch Means Analysis in analyze.py. Divide the 100,000 runs into 10 distinct batches of 10,000. Calculate the median ASI date for each batch. If the inter-batch variance for the median is less than 1 month, the simulation has converged.
Managing Randomness and Scenarios
To ensure the repository is auditable, the random seed must be explicitly controlled. Set a hardcoded seed (e.g., numpy.random.seed(42)) at the beginning of the simulation script. When external researchers clone the repository, they will generate the exact same distributions.
When comparing scenarios (e.g., Fast Takeoff vs. Slow Takeoff), it is critical to use the exact same random seed. This isolates the variance. If you change the ai_rd_automation_lag distribution, using the same seed ensures that the observed delta in the ASI arrival date is caused solely by the parameter shift, rather than random sampling noise.
Avoiding False Precision (Week-Level vs. Month-Level)
A week-level forecast for an event occurring years in the future is statistically indefensible. Given that input variables possess variances spanning several months (e.g., the METR task-horizon doubling time has a 95% CI spanning from ~2 months to 20 months), projecting this compound uncertainty through a multi-stage causal graph guarantees an output distribution spanning multiple years. Presenting a median to the precision of a specific week falsely implies an understanding of micro-dynamics that do not exist in the model.   
Guidance: Month-level forecasting is the absolute maximum honest precision. The repository should round all final output variables to the nearest month (YYYY-MM) and explicitly disclaim that this precision is utilized primarily to visualize smooth probability density curves, not to assert absolute temporal certainty.
Sensitivity Analysis and Validation Plan
A forecast is only as robust as its validation protocols. To prevent the model from operating as a black box that merely obfuscates the author's priors, implement the following analytical tools using Python libraries such as SALib and scikit-learn.
Variance-Based Sensitivity (Sobol Indices): Use SALib to calculate total-order Sobol indices. This answers the critical question: "What percentage of the variance in the ASI arrival date is caused by hardware scaling versus algorithmic efficiency?" Unlike simple correlation, Sobol analysis captures non-linear interactions between variables (e.g., how the AI R&D bottleneck interacts with physical compute limits).
Monotonic Sensitivity (Spearman Rank Correlation): Calculate the Spearman correlation coefficient between every input distribution and the final output date. Present this data as a Tornado Plot in the repository's main README.md. This visually ranks the top 10 forecast drivers, immediately showing observers where the model is most sensitive.
Backtesting against Historical Milestones: Extract the historical state of variables from January 2024. Run the model forward to predict the capability levels of mid-2026. If the model fails to retrodict the achievement of Claude 3.5 Sonnet or the 31x NanoGPT speedrun, its forward-looking ASI predictions are structurally flawed.   
Calibration and Brier Scores: Formulate near-term micro-forecasts derived from the model (e.g., "Probability that SWE-bench Pro exceeds 60% by December 2026"). As these dates pass, calculate the Brier score of the model's probabilities. Over time, this proves whether the model is well-calibrated (scoring close to 0) or overly confident.
Detecting Encoded Priors: Run a null-hypothesis scenario where all parameters are set to uniform distributions with maximum bounds. If the model still outputs the author's preferred timeline, the structural equations themselves are biased.
Public Claim Wording and Prior Intuition
The user holds an intuitive prior that ASI may arrive around July 2029. Directly engineering the model to output this date corrupts the empirical integrity of the repository, turning it into an exercise in motivated reasoning.
To publish the prior honestly, separate the "Author Prior" from the "Empirical Base Case". Maintain the base_forecast_inputs.yaml strictly via data extracted from the trend_inputs.csv and extracted_assumptions.csv. Then, create a separate file, forecast_inputs/author_prior_scenario.yaml, which explicitly documents the specific parameters required to achieve a July 2029 median. The model must be allowed to disagree with the author. If the empirical data pushes the median to 2031, the resulting graphs should plot both probability density curves side-by-side, visually articulating the delta between intuition and evidence.
Desired Public Phrasing Style:
"Based on the hybrid structural extrapolation of current compute scaling, algorithmic efficiency, and task-horizon benchmarks, the current median Empirical Forecast for internal capability-and-agency ASI is October 2031, with a 50% interval of [March 2030 – August 2033] and a 90% interval of [December 2028 – May 2037]. Public visibility of ASI is forecast for April 2032, accounting for an estimated 6-month governance and deployment delay. Separately, the Author's Intuitive Prior scenario yields a median of July 2029. Both outputs are generated dynamically under the assumptions explicitly documented and version-controlled in base_forecast_inputs.yaml (v1.2.4)."
